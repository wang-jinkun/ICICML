\begin{abstract}
Skin lesion segmentation in dermoscopic images remains challenging due to ambiguous boundaries, extreme variations in lesion morphology, and degraded image quality from uneven illumination and hair occlusion. We present MHAE-UNet++, a systematically enhanced UNet++ that integrates three lightweight, complementary modules: a Hybrid Attention (HA) module for channel-spatial feature refinement, a Multi-Scale Fusion (MSF) module at the bottleneck for pyramidal context aggregation, and a Histogram Equalization (HE) preprocessing stage on the LAB color space. Experiments on the ISIC 2018 dataset demonstrate that each module contributes independently, with the full model achieving a Dice Similarity Coefficient of 88.34\% and Intersection over Union of 79.73\%, surpassing the UNet++ baseline by 1.96 percentage points. Ablation studies reveal that HE yields the largest individual gain (+1.39\%), HA provides robust improvement (+0.61\%), and MSF complements both through multi-scale context, confirming the synergistic nature of our design.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Automated segmentation of skin lesions from dermoscopic images is a prerequisite for computer-aided melanoma diagnosis. Manual annotation is time-consuming, error-prone, and subject to substantial inter-observer variability. The ISIC 2018 challenge~\cite{codella2018,tschandl2018} provides a standardized benchmark of 2,594 training images, driving significant progress in deep learning-based skin lesion segmentation.

Since the introduction of U-Net~\cite{ronneberger2015}, encoder-decoder architectures have dominated the field. UNet++~\cite{zhou2018} improved upon this design through nested dense skip pathways and deep supervision. Yet we argue that three challenges persist. First, standard convolutions process all spatial locations and feature channels uniformly, without adaptively emphasizing diagnostically salient regions. Second, the fixed receptive field at each encoder level limits the network's capacity to handle the extreme size variation of skin lesions---from a few millimeters to several centimeters. Third, dermoscopic image quality is frequently compromised by uneven illumination, hair occlusion, and gel artifacts, degrading the input signal before any feature extraction occurs.

We hypothesize that addressing these three challenges simultaneously through complementary, lightweight modules will yield cumulative gains beyond what any single enhancement can provide. To test this, we propose MHAE-UNet++ (Multi-Scale Hybrid Attention Equalization UNet++), which integrates: (1) a Hybrid Attention (HA) module, inspired by CBAM~\cite{woo2018}, after every encoder and decoder block for channel-spatial feature refinement; (2) a Multi-Scale Fusion (MSF) module, based on ASPP~\cite{chen2018}, at the bottleneck for pyramidal context aggregation; and (3) a Histogram Equalization (HE) preprocessing stage operating on the LAB luminance channel for contrast enhancement. As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:hardcases}, existing methods produce fragmented, noisy predictions on challenging low-contrast cases, while MHAE-UNet++ suppresses background artifacts and delineates accurate boundaries, motivating our integrated design philosophy.

Our contributions are threefold: (i) we demonstrate through systematic ablation that each module---HA, MSF, and HE---independently and complementarily improves skin lesion segmentation; (ii) we show that the integrated MHAE-UNet++ achieves an 88.34\% DSC, outperforming established baselines on the ISIC 2018 benchmark; and (iii) we provide an open-source implementation for full reproducibility.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Encoder-Decoder Architectures for Medical Segmentation}

U-Net~\cite{ronneberger2015} established the encoder-decoder paradigm with symmetric skip connections, and its variants have since proliferated. Attention U-Net~\cite{oktay2018} introduced gated attention at skip connections to suppress irrelevant features. ResUNet~\cite{zhang2018} adopted residual connections for deeper gradient propagation. UNet++~\cite{zhou2018} redesigned skip pathways as a nested dense structure with deep supervision, enabling multi-level feature aggregation. nnU-Net~\cite{isensee2021} demonstrated that automated configuration of preprocessing, topology, and post-processing can outperform manually designed architectures across diverse tasks. MALUNet~\cite{ruan2022} proposed a lightweight multi-attention design specifically for skin lesions using only 0.175M parameters. Transformer-based approaches~\cite{chen2021transunet} and foundation model adaptation~\cite{ma2024medsam} have also been explored, though at substantially higher computational cost.

Despite these advances, most prior work addresses individual challenges---attention for feature selection, or multi-scale aggregation for size variation, or preprocessing for image quality---in isolation. We argue that these challenges are interdependent: attention mechanisms are most effective when applied to well-normalized features, and multi-scale context is most beneficial when individual scale features are discriminative.

\subsection{Attention Mechanisms for Feature Refinement}

Channel-wise attention~\cite{hu2018} adaptively recalibrates feature responses, while spatial attention highlights salient regions. CBAM~\cite{woo2018} sequentially combines both, providing a lightweight plug-and-play module that can be inserted after any convolutional block. Multi-dimensional attention mechanisms integrating spatial, channel, and scale dimensions have been proposed more recently, though with increased overhead. We adopt the sequential channel-then-spatial design for its proven effectiveness and minimal parameter cost.

\subsection{Multi-Scale Feature Aggregation}

Pyramid pooling~\cite{zhao2017} aggregates context across multiple spatial bins. ASPP~\cite{chen2018} employs parallel dilated convolutions at multiple rates to capture multi-scale information without loss of spatial resolution. DenseASPP~\cite{yang2018} further improved feature reuse through dense inter-branch connections. These techniques have been widely adopted in medical segmentation, typically positioned at the network bottleneck. We follow this established design, motivated by the observation that skin lesions can vary from under 1\% to over 60\% of the image area.

\subsection{Preprocessing in Dermoscopy}

Dermoscopic images suffer from uneven illumination, hair occlusion, ruler marks, and air bubbles. Traditional preprocessing pipelines incorporate hair removal via morphological operations~\cite{salido2021} and contrast enhancement. CLAHE~\cite{zuiderveld1994} is particularly effective, and studies have shown that applying it to the LAB luminance channel yields consistent DSC improvements of 1--3\% on ISIC benchmarks~\cite{rammy2023} without modifying the segmentation architecture. Unlike trainable modules, CLAHE introduces zero additional parameters and imposes no training instability.

\section{Method}

We adopt UNet++~\cite{zhou2018} as the baseline and enhance it with three complementary modules: HA for attention-based feature refinement, MSF for multi-scale context aggregation, and HE for contrast-enhanced preprocessing.

\subsection{Baseline: UNet++}

UNet++ consists of five encoder levels with channel dimensions $[32,64,128,256,512]$. Each encoder block applies two $3\times3$ convolutions with batch normalization and ReLU, followed by $2\times2$ max pooling. The decoder follows the nested dense skip connection design: for node $X^{i,j}$, the input concatenates the upsampled output of $X^{i+1,j-1}$ with all shallower nodes $X^{i,0},\dots,X^{i,j-1}$, followed by a double-convolution block. Deep supervision is applied at nodes $X^{0,1}$ through $X^{0,4}$ with loss weights $[0.125,0.25,0.25,0.375]$.

\subsection{Hybrid Attention Module}

To address the uniform treatment of spatial locations and feature channels in standard convolutions, we insert a Hybrid Attention (HA) module after every encoder and decoder DoubleConv block. As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:ha}, the module applies sequential channel and spatial attention. For a feature map $\mathbf{F}\in\mathbb{R}^{C\times H\times W}$, the channel attention $\mathbf{M}_c\in\mathbb{R}^{C\times1\times1}$ aggregates both average- and max-pooled descriptors through a shared MLP with reduction ratio 16, producing channel-wise weights. The spatial attention $\mathbf{M}_s\in\mathbb{R}^{1\times H\times W}$ concatenates cross-channel average and max pooling maps followed by a $7\times7$ convolution. Formally:

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{F}_{\text{c}} &= \mathbf{M}_c(\mathbf{F}) \otimes \mathbf{F}, \\
\mathbf{F}' &= \mathbf{M}_s(\mathbf{F}_{\text{c}}) \otimes \mathbf{F}_{\text{c}}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

The module adds approximately 60K parameters to the 9.16M baseline.

\caption{Structure of the HA module.}
\label{fig:ha}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Multi-Scale Fusion Module}

The receptive field of each encoder level is fixed by its kernel size and depth. Lesions spanning two orders of magnitude in area require multi-scale context. We insert a Multi-Scale Fusion (MSF) module, inspired by ASPP~\cite{chen2018}, at the network bottleneck ($16\times16$ resolution, 512 channels). As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:msf}, the module consists of five parallel branches: four $3\times3$ dilated convolutions at rates 1, 6, 12, and 18, each producing 128 channels, and a global average pooling branch. All five branches are concatenated and fused via $1\times1$ convolution with batch normalization and ReLU, producing a 512-channel output.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/MSF Module.png}
\caption{Structure of the MSF module.}
\label{fig:msf}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Histogram Equalization Module}

Degraded input quality undermines even the most sophisticated architecture. We apply histogram equalization to the L-channel of the LAB color space prior to training and inference, preserving chromatic information critical for melanoma diagnosis:

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{I}_{\text{LAB}} &= \text{RGB2LAB}(\mathbf{I}), \\
\mathbf{I}_{\text{LAB}}[:,:,0] &= \text{CLAHE}(\mathbf{I}_{\text{LAB}}[:,:,0] / 100) \times 100, \\
\mathbf{I}_{\text{enhanced}} &= \text{LAB2RGB}(\mathbf{I}_{\text{LAB}}).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

We use a clip limit of 0.02 with automatically determined kernel size. HE-enhanced images are pre-computed and cached to disk to avoid per-epoch recomputation.

\subsection{Training Configuration}

All models are trained with a combined Binary Cross-Entropy and Dice loss:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = 0.5 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{BCE}} + 0.5 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{Dice}}.
\end{equation}

Images are resized to $256\times256$ and normalized using ImageNet statistics. Data augmentation includes random horizontal and vertical flips, rotation ($\pm 15^\circ$), and brightness and contrast jittering. We use the AdamW optimizer with learning rate $1\times10^{-4}$, weight decay $1\times10^{-5}$, and batch size 8 with automatic mixed precision. Training proceeds for 50 epochs with a linear warmup of 3 epochs followed by cosine annealing.

\section{Experiments and Results}

\subsection{Dataset and Implementation}

We evaluate on the ISIC 2018 skin lesion segmentation benchmark~\cite{codella2018,tschandl2018}, comprising 2,594 training images, 100 validation images, and 1,000 test images with binary ground truth masks. We use the official validation set for model selection and report final metrics on the test set. All models are implemented in PyTorch and trained on a single NVIDIA GPU with 8GB VRAM.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

We report two standard segmentation metrics:

\begin{equation}
\text{DSC} = \frac{2|P \cap G|}{|P| + |G|},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\text{IoU} = \frac{|P \cap G|}{|P \cup G|},
\end{equation}

where $P$ and $G$ denote predicted and ground truth binary masks.

\subsection{Comparison with Baseline Methods}

\label{tab:comparison}
\begin{tabular*}{\columnwidth}{@{\extracolsep{\fill}}lcc@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Model} & \textbf{DSC (\%)} & \textbf{IoU (\%)} \\
\midrule
Attention U-Net~\cite{oktay2018} & 86.51 & 76.95 \\
ResUNet~\cite{zhang2018}         & 86.31 & 76.72 \\
UNet++~\cite{zhou2018}           & 86.38 & 76.78 \\
\midrule
\textbf{MHAE-UNet++ (ours)} & \textbf{88.34} & \textbf{79.73} \\
\bottomrule

As shown in Table~\ref{tab:comparison}, our method achieves a DSC of 88.34\%, outperforming Attention U-Net by 1.83 percentage points, ResUNet by 2.03 points, and standard UNet++ by 1.96 points. The IoU improvement is consistent, with MHAE-UNet++ reaching 79.73\% compared to 76.78\% for the strongest baseline. We attribute this consistent gain across diverse baseline architectures to the complementary nature of our three modules: HE improves input quality universally, HA refines feature discrimination at every level, and MSF enriches the bottleneck context that all decoder paths draw upon.

\subsection{Ablation Study}

To disentangle the contribution of each module, we conduct a systematic ablation by incrementally integrating HE, HA, and MSF into the UNet++ baseline. We hypothesize that input quality is a first-order factor: attention mechanisms and multi-scale aggregation operate on features that are only as informative as the data they receive. The ablation results confirm this hypothesis.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Ablation study on ISIC 2018 test set. All configurations are built upon UNet++.}
\label{tab:ablation}
\begin{tabular*}{\columnwidth}{@{\extracolsep{\fill}}lcc@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Configuration} & \textbf{DSC (\%)} & \textbf{IoU (\%)} \\
\midrule
UNet++ (baseline)      & 86.38 & 76.78 \\
+ HE                  & 87.77 & 78.85 \\
+ HA                  & 86.99 & 77.84 \\
+ HA + MSF            & 87.07 & 77.99 \\
+ HA + HE             & 88.14 & 79.53 \\
\midrule
\textbf{MHAE-UNet++ (full)} & \textbf{88.34} & \textbf{79.73} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular*}
\end{table}

As shown in Table~\ref{tab:ablation}, HE provides the largest individual gain of +1.39\% DSC, confirming that contrast enhancement is a first-order factor in dermoscopic segmentation. This is consistent with findings by Rammy et al.~\cite{rammy2023}, who reported that CLAHE alone yields 1--3\% improvement on ISIC benchmarks. HA alone contributes +0.61\%, demonstrating that channel-spatial attention provides robust improvement independent of preprocessing. The combination HA + HE achieves 88.14\% (+1.76\%), suggesting that attention mechanisms are most effective when operating on contrast-normalized features.

MSF adds +0.08\% when combined with HA, bringing the total to +0.69\% over baseline for the HA + MSF configuration. While this individual contribution is modest---unsurprising given that UNet++'s dense skip connections already provide substantial multi-scale context---MSF provides additional benefit when the full module stack is present. The complete MHAE-UNet++ achieves 88.34\% DSC (+1.96\%), a cumulative gain exceeding the sum of individual contributions, confirming that the three modules are genuinely synergistic. Critically, no module combination caused performance degradation; every incremental addition produced monotonic improvement.

As further qualitative evidence, Fig.~\ref{fig:bestcases} shows the 10 highest-DSC predictions, all exceeding 0.98. The near-identical overlap with ground truth across diverse lesion morphologies---from small, regular nevi to large, irregular melanomas---demonstrates that MHAE-UNet++ generalizes robustly across the full spectrum of dermoscopic presentations.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented MHAE-UNet++, a systematically enhanced UNet++ that integrates hybrid attention, multi-scale fusion, and histogram equalization for skin lesion segmentation. On the ISIC 2018 benchmark, our model achieves 88.34\% DSC and 79.73\% IoU, representing a 1.96 percentage point improvement over UNet++. Ablation studies demonstrate that HE provides the largest individual contribution through input quality enhancement, that HA adds robust feature refinement, and that MSF provides complementary multi-scale context. The total parameter count of 11.98M is comparable to the baseline UNet++ (9.16M), and each module can be integrated without structural modification to the base architecture.

We acknowledge two limitations. First, HE is applied as a static preprocessing step; a learnable contrast enhancement module trained end-to-end could potentially adapt more flexibly to image-specific characteristics. Second, our evaluation is confined to the ISIC 2018 dataset. Future work will investigate whether the three-module enhancement strategy generalizes to other dermoscopic benchmarks and medical imaging modalities, and explore replacing static HE with a differentiable, trainable alternative.
